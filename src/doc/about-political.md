# My Politics

## Leftism

### Anarchist Communism
Ideologically I am an Anarcho-communist, which is a shortening of the term “Anarchist Communist” — a sub-ideology of Libertarian Socialism. Usually when people here either of these two terms, and especially when combined, their mind immediately jumps to:

1. Scary evil word and *another* scary evil word!
2. Wait isn't that an oxymoron?

This thought comes from a misconception of what the two terms are. <b style="color: red;">Communism</b> is a political ideology that desires to reach a stateless, classless, moneyless, and totally egalitarian society. <b style="color: var(--light-gray);">Anarchism</b> is a political ideology that seeks abolish any and all forms of hierarchy, authority, and coercion to build a stateless society centred on the idea of free mutual exchange and association. <b style="color: var(--light-gray);">Anarcho</b>-<b style="color: red;">Communism</b> is a synthesis of the two – seeking to establish a communist society by first creating one that is free from *both* capitalism *and* the state.

Unlike other leftist ideologies, anarcho-communism is fundamentally sceptical of using the state to achieve communism. The state, being a hierarchical institution, is always going to be: a tool of oppression; a means of dividing society; opposed to non-reformist sociopolitical changes; and opposed to its own deconstruction/abolition. We see this with both former and modern Marxist-Leninist or post-Marxist regimes. The inner members of the vanguard party end up becoming their own class of bureaucratic elites, unable or unwilling to hear to the voices of the masses.

This doesn't mean that liberal democracy gets a free pass – representative democracy, even if socialist, still is a deeply flawed system. Large groups of people cannot agree on the same thing, and without consensus you open the door to tyranny of either the minority or the majority. Additionally, representatives without instant-recollection are always going to be more incentivised by external forces than by their own people.

Of course, like all leftist ideologies, capitalism is bad, and significantly worse than the state – at least you can vote out your senator, in a corporation you have very little influence over board members.

For at least the last 80 years, thanks to the Industrial Revolution, we have had more than enough food, land, materials, and machinery to be able to make every single person on this planet well-fed, housed, healthy etc. We have made massive strides in agriculture and medicine, from GMOs to antibiotics. The very fact that we still live in a world where people go hungry and sick because they can’t afford to access these resources should be considered the greatest failure of capitalist civilisation.

The core idea of socialism is to make the workplace democratic, to ensure that everybody is given what they need, and to not put a price on life itself.

### Misconceptions on Historical Socialism

Despite my dislike of current “Communist” regimes, it is important to point out that our view of regimes from modern China to the USSR has been heavily warped by cold-war rhetoric and propaganda.

Most regimes commonly associated with communism aren't actually communist, or even socialist. Most notably being the USSR after Lenin and China in its entirety. These regimes are best labelled as “Authoritarian Collectivist” instead, as they are at best approximations of socialism, yet too authoritarian for there to be any real workplace democracy. Ironically the USSR under Gorbachev was closer to socialism than it was under Stalin because the late USSR was more dominated by worker cooperatives.

But irrespective of whether these nations were socialist, keep in mind is that past leftist regimes were not authoritarian on purpose. These societies were subject to a constant and overwhelming global crusade against the very idea of socialism. We love to point out the authoritarianism of people like Vladimir Lenin, Ho Chi Minh, and Fidel Castro – but also never want to point out that in all three cases they were leading a nation under attack from all sides.

The early USSR was in the middle of a civil war, being sabotaged at every turn by every major European power. Ever since its revolution, Cuba has been continually under America's *strictest* embargo for 70 years now. Vietnam had spent the majority of its existence in the 20th century fighting off foreign invaders.

For example, did you know that Cuba has been a democracy since 2006? Or that Leninist Russia had legalised homosexuality and had begun researching gender-affirming surgeries? Or what about Burkina Faso's radical economic improvements under Sankara? Or anarchist Ukraine's agricultural production being so large that it couldn't export the excess fast enough?

Does this justify a lot of the atrocities committed by these regimes? No. While western propaganda often over-exaggerates the scale and amount of atrocities (for example the “100 billion” figure is a complete falsehood), it doesn't mean that what *did* happen was good.

### Misconceptions on Anarchism

These arguments levelled against Marxist-Leninist regimes are often levelled against Anarchist societies as well — except on overdrive. The classic repetitive arguments of “it doesn't work”, “it's doomed to fail”, “humans just aren't built for it”, etc.

All of these arguments often fall into the vein of three categories:
1. A misunderstanding of how an anarchist society operates
2. Capitalism realism
3. Statist realism

The first point is mainly due to a lack of education. Anarchism is not chaos, and it is not about letting people do whatever. Anarchism, at its core, is about consent. It seeks to build *structures* and *governments* that operate on a principle of consensual governance and mutual aid. Anarcho-communist societies organise themselves around interconnected and overlapping collectives – communes, cooperatives, councils – where people are free to associate with whichever will aid them in living life to the fullest.

The next two points originate from a deep misconception about human nature. Capitalist realism assumes that capitalism is the best viable system because humans are naturally selfish, greedy, and individualistic. Statist realism assumes that the state is a practical necessary to maintain order and organise large-scale social structures. Both these beliefs fail to consider how the systems in which we live in effect our development and actions; humans are naturally altruistic, capitalism itself is what motivates excessive greed; humans are naturally cooperative – the state is what encourages delinquency.
